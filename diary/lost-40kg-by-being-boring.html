I lost 40kg by being boring

The story people want is a turning point — the photo, the rock bottom, the morning it all changed. There wasn't one. I lost forty kilograms the boring way: by making the default choice slightly better, then not thinking about it again for two years.

Everyone treats weight as a willpower problem, which is exactly why everyone loses the same five kilos every January. Willpower is a terrible technology — expensive, unreliable, and gone by 9pm. Systems are cheap and they compound. I didn't decide to eat well every day; I decided once what "well" looked like, removed the decision, and let the boredom do the work. Atomic Habits is unfashionable to recommend and entirely right about this one thing: you don't rise to your goals, you fall to your systems.

The part nobody puts in the montage is that it's dull. No daily triumph, no transformation arc — just the same unremarkable choices repeated until the body quietly catches up. From the outside the result looks like discipline. From the inside it mostly felt like having stopped negotiating with myself every single day.

And the forty kilos was never really the point. It was proof — to me, more than anyone — that I could change something at the identity level if I made it boring enough to sustain. That's the only self-improvement lesson I actually trust: the dramatic version is for telling at dinner; the boring version is the one that works.
